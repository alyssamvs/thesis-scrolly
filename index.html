LAWS OF A FOREVER WAR

Visualising repressive drug-related policies and their impact on the global cocaine trade.

In the 1860s, a beverage made out of wine and coca leaves was marketed around the world as a cure for a wide variety of conditions: to stay awake, to increase energy, to alleviate pain and enhance artistic and athletic performance. ‘Vin Mariani’, so named after its creator, a French chemist, was endorsed by popes, drank by celebrities, and later inspired the creation of ‘Coca Cola’.


It contained around 220mg of cocaine per litre.

At that time, no prohibition existed on coca leaves and the alkaloid contained within them, cocaine. In fact, no prohibition existed for any type of drug, at least not in a way that could be considered comprehensive. Coca was popular and beloved: in the Andes mountains, its leaves had been chewed and ground for energy and pain relief by indigenous people since 8000 B.C.E., then after its chemical isolation in 1855 it spread across the globe, used as anesthetic, herbal liqueur, energy drink and more.

Despite some efforts in the early 20th century to regulate or ban it, cocaine manufacture and processing plants in Peru and Bolivia boomed, with both countries prepared to make the most of the plant's potential.

Then, just after the end of World War II, it all came to an end.

In January, 1912, the first treaty to establish drug prohibitions at the international level was approved in the Hague. This was a treatie borne out of conflict.

Made under the auspices of the League of Nations, countries agreed to 'perform their best efforts to control the manufacture, import, sale, distribution and export of morphine, cocaine, and their derivatives'.

In most cases, though, those 'best efforts' were few and far between, and even by its signatories, the treaty was largely ignored.
