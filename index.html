1. Cocaine: An Abridged History

In the 1860s, a beverage made out of wine and coca leaves was marketed around the world as a cure-all: you could take it to stay awake, to increase energy, to alleviate pain and enhance artistic and athletic performance. ‘Vin Mariani’, so named after its creator, a French chemist, was endorsed by popes, drank by celebrities, and enjoyed by millions.

It contained around 220mg of cocaine per litre.

Coca plants (Erythroxylum coca) are shrub-like bushes native to western South America. In the Andes mountains, their leaves have been chewed and ground for energy and pain relief by indigenous people since 8000 B.C.E., typically with the addition of calcite or lime so that the alkaloid it contains may be absorbed. The alkaloid in question: cocaine.

At the time of Vin Mariani's production, no prohibition existed on coca leaves and cocaine. In fact, no prohibition existed for any type of drug, at least not in any way that could be considered comprehensive. So, same as with opium products, cocaine was freely used and beloved: following its chemical isolation in 1855 it spread across the globe, and began to be added to medicine, drinks, tonics, mood enhancing elixirs, and even foods. Sigmund Freud famously advocated for its use, and it was an ingredient in the original recipe for Coca Cola until 1904.

By the turn of the 20th century, some efforts had been made to curb its use, owing in no small part to its association with crimes (real or perceived) committed under its influence by the African American population in the United States. In January 1912, the League of Nations approved the first treaty to establish drug prohibitions at the international level. However, despite multiple ammendments and expanding protocols, it did not see widespread enforcement.

By the late 1940s, cocaine manufacture and processing plants in Peru and Bolivia had boomed, with both countries prepared to make the most of the plant's potential and a booming, globalised market.

Then, in 1949, it all came to an end.

3. A Legal System Catches Up

As mentioned, drug regulation in general is a relatively new phenomenom. The first document to declare at an international level the necessity to intervene in the commerce of certain substances dates back to 1909. Following the Second World War, a wave of prohibition instigated mainly by the United States of America lead to widespread pressure for countries to abide by these provisions.

It took until 1961, however, for these treaties to have any teeth.

They haven't been updated much since then.

This is a network showing most major international legislation related to drug control. The process, on paper, is relatively simple: countries sign the treaties, their governments ratify them, and once in force, they are enacted into law. In reality, it's slightly trickier than that.

Let's take as an example the Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs -- currently one of three foundational UN conventions for international drug control. Both Peru and the United States are parties to this convention; however, they ratified it three years apart.

In Peru, the Convention was signed in 1961 with an indication that they would not be subject to the prohibition of traditional uses of coca leaf. This is called a reservation.

However, on account of international pressure, this reservation was withdrawn at the moment the treaty was ratified.

More than ten years later, Peru would finally adopt the text of the Convention into law.

Similarly, upon signing the 1971 Vienna Convention on Psychotropic Substances, the United States made a reservation excluding the prohibition of the cactus Peyote in its traditional use.

Since 2012, however, Cannabis has been legalised at the state level without the US presenting any further reservation to the Convention.

In short, nowadays a multitude of similar international pieces of legislation exist: at the UN, regional, and local level, with varying degrees of adherance, and that each country may individually modify or update when codifying it into local law.

There is something they all share, though: their focus on control. And though drug control is an issue of demand as well as supply, the overwhelming amount of the legislation that exists today addresses only the supply side.
